## STAR Format

Structure answers as: **Situation** (context), **Task** (your responsibility), **Action** (what you did), **Result** (outcome).

Keep answers to 1-2 minutes. Be specific. End with what you learned or would do differently.

### 1. Turning around the 40+ developer team at Banco Galicia (People leadership / growing engineers)
**S:** As a consultancy (Flux IT), we had 40+ mobile and backend developers embedded at Banco Galicia, maintaining the bank's mobile app and its backend. The team was struggling on the human side: high turnover, many developers without a career plan, seniors overstretched, juniors without effective mentoring, low code quality and a high bug count, velocity below the client's expectations, friction with the client, and little sense of belonging to the consultancy. The clearest signal: **63% of the developers who resigned had never been evaluated for a seniority review** — people were leaving before anyone even looked at their growth.

**T:** I was the Technical Leader **not assigned to a single squad**, but responsible for accompanying all 40+ developers at the client. My explicit mandate, facing the developers, was to support their growth, drive technical and process improvement, define and enforce best practices, and set objectives. I worked in coordination with a Project Leader (management side) and an HRBP (HR-tooling side).

**A:** I led with people first:
- Ran **1:1s with every developer**, listening to their real needs (wanting to move, salary concerns, no growth vision) and mapping paths forward. I became the go-to reference for both technical and personal project matters.
- Built **technology communities** (Java backend, Android, iOS) so knowledge flowed across squads instead of staying siloed.
- Delivered cross-cutting training for everyone (git, code reviews, engineering best practices).
- Two cases that show the approach: **(1)** I took a semi-senior developer and built a career plan from *his own* interests, with measurable objectives, follow-up, and coaching conversations about where he wanted to go, and he made **Senior**. **(2)** A Java developer who kept clashing with the client's leaders and had asked to switch teams twice; I saw he couldn't process conflict, so through 1:1s and **Ontological Coaching tools** I helped him work through it, learn to hold hard conversations with the client, and strengthen his profile. He was later promoted to **Technical Leader and replaced me** when I moved to another client.

**R:** People outcomes led the way:
- **Recovered turnover:** it started at 17%, spiked above 30% during the first year (a hard stretch), and the 1:1s, career plans and communities brought it back down to the original 17%. (Honest framing: this is a recovery to baseline after a spike, not a reduction below it.)
- **Two developers I coached were promoted** (semi-senior → senior; and the conflict-averse Java dev → Technical Leader who took over my role).
- Regular 1:1s and career plans became the norm, directly attacking the "63% left without a seniority review" problem.
- The tech communities took hold: within a year, **30% of developers** were presenting and sharing knowledge with peers across squads.
- Upskilling on cutting-edge tech: **45% of iOS devs** trained in SwiftUI and **60% of Android devs** in Jetpack Compose.
- Delivery: teams increased velocity by an estimated **~15% in 3 months** (estimated, not formally measured, so present it as an estimate); peer code review reached **90%** of code; adopted SonarLint locally and SonarQube in CI; reinforced SOLID and 12-factor practices.

**Use for:** "Tell me about a time you grew or developed someone", "How do you handle conflict or an underperformer", "Your biggest leadership challenge", "How do you turn around a struggling / low-morale team", "How do you build career paths and run 1:1s", "How do you raise engineering quality across a team"

### 2. Leading the omnichannel microservices squad at Banco Galicia (Leadership in a demanding client culture)
**S:** Banco Galicia launched a full build of a new services layer to expose bank operations across channels (mobile app, individual-customer web, business-customer web, and internal customer-service tools). Flux IT stood up a squad, technically led by me, with 3 backend developers and 1 QA Automation engineer, owning a specific subdomain of the new platform.
**T:** I was responsible for defining best practices, ensuring the team delivered the features specified by an Architect and Analysts, guiding the team to meet deadlines, and safeguarding product quality.
**A:** I ran 1:1 follow-ups with each member, defined the team's way of working within the client's guidelines, and defined the integrations with other systems together with the Architect. The real challenge was cultural: we worked on-site at the client, alongside an Architect and analysts from *other* consultancies, under bank leaders with a very demanding, high-control culture and little freedom to organize the team our way. That produced friction and complaints. Rather than fight it head-on, I focused on shaping a team organization that genuinely served the developers. I showed that better organization raised each member's ownership, so when extra effort was needed it came proactively from the team instead of being imposed.
**R:** Despite a rigid, externally-controlled environment, the squad delivered its subdomain with higher commitment and ownership, extra effort emerged voluntarily from the team, and we demonstrated to the client that a developer-centric organization improved delivery.
**Use for:** "Leading in a demanding or regulated client culture", "Working with stakeholders you don't control", "Building ownership in a team", "Delivering under heavy external control"

### 3. A time I failed as a leader: missing the team's velocity signal (Self-awareness / learning from mistakes)
**S:** Same omnichannel microservices project at Banco Galicia, leading a squad of 3 devs and 1 QA on a demanding, high-control client site.
**T:** As technical lead I was responsible for the team's delivery pace and for surfacing risks early.
**A:** I got absorbed in day-to-day delivery and best practices and stopped watching the team's velocity closely. I only took corrective action once members from the client and from another consultancy pointed the slowdown out to me, not before. Once aware, I owned it, dug into the causes, and changed how we tracked and protected the team's pace.
**R:** We recovered the pace, but the real lesson stuck: a leader's job is to watch the signals the team can't see for itself, and to catch them before a stakeholder does. Since then I build explicit pace and health checkpoints into how I lead, rather than assuming that "heads-down delivery" means everything is fine.
**Use for:** "Tell me about a time you failed as a leader", "A mistake you made and what you learned", "How do you catch problems early", "Receiving hard feedback"

### 4. Turning around an underperformer by leading with the person (Managing performance with empathy)
**S:** On the same Banco Galicia microservices squad, one developer's performance dropped noticeably.
**T:** As his lead I had to address the performance gap without breaking trust or pushing him further down.
**A:** Instead of opening with the performance problem, I opened with the person. I met him 1:1 and simply asked how he was. That surfaced a personal situation he was going through. I offered concrete support: time off if he needed it, and a genuine willingness to listen and accompany him. I deliberately never framed the conversation as "your performance is low."
**R:** Once he worked through the personal issue, he came back with *more* responsibility and commitment than before, and his performance recovered on its own, without a single direct "you're underperforming" conversation. It reinforced how I manage: address the human first, and the performance usually follows.
**Use for:** "How do you handle an underperformer", "A time you supported a struggling team member", "Balancing empathy and accountability", "Coaching and difficult conversations"

### 5. Escalating a disagreement over unrealistic deadlines (Managing up / principled conflict)
**S:** On a project where I was Tech Lead, the Project Leader set delivery dates without asking for or considering estimates, so the committed dates were consistently missed because nothing had actually been estimated.
**T:** I felt responsible for protecting both the client relationship and the team from unrealistic commitments, and for putting the delivery process on honest footing.
**A:** I raised the issue directly with the Project Leader. We couldn't align: he was focused on committing to the client no matter what, while I wanted a realistic path that didn't over-stress the team or promise the impossible. Rather than let it fester or simply comply, I escalated it to my hierarchical leader outside the project and convened a meeting with the account's leaders to review the situation and set new working agreements.
**R:** The escalation moved the problem from a two-person standoff to a leadership-level decision, producing new shared agreements among the account's leaders on how estimates and commitments would be handled. My takeaway: escalate principled disagreements early and through the right channel, and frame them as fixing the process, not as a personal fight.
**Use for:** "A disagreement with your manager or leader", "A time you pushed back on an unrealistic ask", "How you handle conflict", "Managing up", "Protecting your team"

## Questions You Should Ask Interviewers

### About the Role
- "What does a typical week look like in this role?"
- "What would success look like in the first 6 months?"
- "What's the biggest challenge the team is facing right now?"

### About the Team
- "How big is the team, and how do you divide work?"
- "What does the development/project lifecycle look like, from idea to production?"
- "How do you onboard new team members?"

### About Tech & Growth
- "What's your current tech stack for [relevant area]?"
- "Is there room to grow into more architectural or strategic decisions?"
- "How does the team stay current with new tools and methods?"

### About Culture (use these to prevent disappointment)
- "How would you describe the team culture?"
- "What does professional development look like here?"
- "Is there flexibility for remote/hybrid work?"
- "What's the balance between development/new projects and maintenance work?"
- "How would you describe the leadership style in this team?"
- "What do people who thrive here have in common?"

## Phone/Video Interview Tips
- Have STAR examples written out (use this file)
- Keep a glass of water nearby
- Smile when speaking (it changes your tone)
- Ask for clarification if a question is vague
- It's OK to take 5 seconds to think before answering
- End with: "Is there anything else you'd like to know about my background?"

## After the Application (Best Practice)

### Follow-Up Etiquette
- **Don't call to "stand out"** or to learn more about the role post-submission - this risks a negative impression
- If the employer specified a timeline, respect it and wait
- If no timeline was given and significant time has passed (2+ weeks), a brief call to ask about status is acceptable
- If you have genuinely new, relevant information to share, a short follow-up is fine

### Thank-You Notes
- When you receive any update (interview invitation, rejection, or status update), send a brief thank-you message
- Express appreciation for their time and the process
- Keep it short (2-3 sentences)

## Roleplay Guidelines
When the user asks for interview practice:
1. Ask which role/company to simulate
2. Start with easy warm-up questions ("Tell me about yourself")
3. Progress to role-specific technical questions
4. Include 1-2 behavioral questions using the competencies from the job posting
5. End with a tough question or curveball
6. After each answer, give brief feedback: what worked, what to sharpen
7. Suggest which STAR example would work best for each question
